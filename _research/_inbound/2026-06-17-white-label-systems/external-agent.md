# 33-white-label-systems

## 1. White-label as an engagement type — meta-framing and altitude

Within the practitioner-facing discipline of architecting and operating design systems at a global creative agency scale, the concept of "white-label" is frequently miscategorized as a purely technical challenge. Organizations often conflate it with advanced multi-brand token architecture or view it merely as a specialized variant of theming. This conflation obscures the true nature of the discipline. Multi-brand represents the structural architecture—specifically relying on multi-collection-not-multi-mode configurations for brand semantics, per the established token tree strategies. In stark contrast, white-label represents the distinct shape of the engagement that the practice scopes, prices, signs, and delivers to the client. The architecture and the engagement shape operate on entirely different axes; a practice that only articulates the multi-brand architecture will systematically under-scope the commercial engagement, absorbing the operational overhead of brand onboarding without capturing its recurring revenue.

In the practitioner's vault, this white-label engagement framing sits directly alongside and functions as an extension topic to token architecture extensions (22), tokens at scale (24), adaptive interfaces (26/27), generated-from-data documentation (30), color systems (31), auditing design systems (32), and Model Context Protocol (MCP) implementations for design system practice (34). Specifically, white-label-as-an-engagement-type serves as the fourth productisation companion alongside AI-readiness (30), auditing (32), and workstation MCPs (34). It follows the identical productisation template: it defines a workstation-vs-engagement-vs-portfolio billing distinction, explicitly articulates the cost of not having the operational framework, and ships a synthesis-ready operational artifact. However, it applies this template to a completely different slice of practice intellectual property, focusing on the commercial and operational mechanics of serving unknown future brands.

The distinction between pitching a "multi-brand design system" and pitching a "white-label design system" is the primary wedge that unlocks recurring commercial value. An agency pitching a multi-brand architecture typically prices the token taxonomy, the component configuration, and the initial rollout, but entirely omits the engagement shape required to sustain it at scale. Consequently, the resulting statement of work (SOW) lacks provisions for continuous brand-onboarding, features no retainer for the maintenance of the underlying brand-generation engine, and establishes no per-brand pricing model. Conversely, pitching a white-label design system explicitly scopes both the underlying token architecture and the operational engagement. It frames the programmatic brand-generation engine as a distinct, billable Phase 2 deliverable, positions the brand-onboarding runbook as a formalized asset, and establishes recurring brand-onboarding revenue as a long-term retainer line. The per-brand SOW shape becomes a productised template, transforming a static architectural artifact into a dynamic, recurring revenue stream.

The cost of miscategorization in this space is profound and financially detrimental. A practice that conflates white-label engagements with standard multi-brand builds inevitably under-prices both, assuming the heavy operational overhead of the former while charging exclusively for the static delivery of the latter. Furthermore, conflating a white-label system built specifically for resellers with a white-label system built as an agency starter foundation results in shipping the wrong architecture for the brief. Building a fully automated brand-generation engine for an internal starter library that does not strictly require one represents severe over-engineering and a massive sunk cost. Conversely, shipping an opinionated, un-parameterized starter library to a client running a reseller platform will result in an architecture that inherently fails to scale past three or four tenants, triggering cascading technical debt, visual fragmentation, and a localized collapse of the system's governance model. Articulating the two distinct shapes of white-label engagements is the only strategic mechanism that prevents these critical miscategorizations.

## 2. The two white-label shapes — for-resellers vs. as-starter

The operational discipline necessitates a strict, uncompromising bifurcation of white-label engagements into two distinct shapes: "white-label-for-resellers" and "white-label-as-starter." Selecting the correct shape during initial client discovery dictates the subsequent architectural choices, the required headless-vs-opinionated component split, the staffing model, and the commercial SOW.

The first shape, white-label-for-resellers, is defined by a single core product facilitating an arbitrary, theoretically infinite number of reseller-supplied brand inputs. The operational flow dictates that a reseller signs up for the platform and supplies a minimum necessary brand set—such as a logo, a primary color hex code, a typeface stack, and a voice tone parameter. These inputs serve as the data payload for the Theme Schema contract. The underlying system's brand-generation engine processes these inputs and mechanically derives the remainder of the brand's semantic layer, including contrast-pinned color ramps, interaction states, and typographic scales. The end-user product remains structurally and functionally identical across all resellers; only the brand presentation layer varies.

Prominent examples of this shape in the field include Shopify's theme marketplace, which leverages the Polaris design tokens to dynamically theme interfaces, Mailchimp's email-template rendering engines, and B2B SaaS platforms providing embedded white-label solutions, such as Stripe Connect's Embedded Components which utilize a highly structured Elements Appearance API to inject variables like `colorPrimary` and `borderRadius` across isolated DOM environments. Architecturally, this shape demands a robust programmatic brand-generation engine, a strictly enforced Theme Schema contract serialized in JSON, a comprehensive semantic-layer token topology, and a formalized reseller onboarding pipeline. Commercially, the for-resellers shape is predicated entirely on recurring brand-onboarding revenue, typically structured as a per-reseller fee upon signup and a monthly retainer for continuous engine access, schema validation, and uptime guarantees.

The second shape, white-label-as-starter, functions fundamentally as a foundational kit and starter component library designed specifically to accelerate the initial phases of bespoke client engagements. The agency ships an internal kit—such as VML's Prism2—where each new client engagement inherits this kit as a baseline architectural foundation. As the engagement progresses through design and development, client-specific customizations systematically diverge from the starter foundation. The end-state is a fully bespoke, highly optimized client design system that benefited immensely from the kit's acceleration but is no longer structurally bound to it or governed by its future updates.

Industry examples of the as-starter shape include IBM Garage's internal starter kits, Pentagram's brand-system templates, and generic agency accelerators that provide foundational tokens and common component layouts. Architecturally, this shape requires a robust starter token set, a starter component library built with best-practice autolayout and state properties, a starter documentation pattern, and strict agency-internal governance to manage the kit's independent evolution without breaking active client builds. Commercially, the value proposition lies strictly in internal agency velocity: the kit significantly reduces the engagement startup cost because the foundational tokens and base components do not begin from zero. However, it explicitly does not generate recurring revenue directly from clients, as it remains an agency-internal asset used for margin compression, consistency, and practitioner velocity, rather than a licensed multi-tenant platform.

The conflation cost between these two shapes is severe. The white-label-for-resellers shape mandates a brand-generation engine because the total list of brands is unknown at the time of architecture and will grow dynamically over the product's lifespan. The engine is a load-bearing, highly complex investment that amortizes over time. The white-label-as-starter shape absolutely does not require this engine. For an as-starter engagement, the brand is known at the time of architecture (it is the single bespoke client's brand), and the transition from kit-to-bespoke occurs manually via design and engineering during the engagement phases. Building a programmatic brand-generation engine for a starter library is a gross misallocation of engineering hours. Conversely, shipping an opinionated, manually themed starter library to a client building a reseller platform will fatally constrain their ability to scale.

To determine the correct shape, the practice must ask a strict set of decision questions during discovery:

- Is the exhaustive list of brands known at architecture time? (If no, the engagement points toward the for-resellers shape).
- Does the system serve a single product with multiple brand instances, or multiple distinct products derived from a single brand foundation? (A single product with multiple brands points toward for-resellers; multiple products derived from a single foundation points toward as-starter).
- Is the generation of recurring revenue from continuous brand-onboarding the primary commercial premise of the engagement, or is the goal to reduce per-engagement internal startup costs? (Recurring revenue points toward for-resellers; startup-cost reduction points toward as-starter).

Each pair of answers points unequivocally to one shape over the other.

While a hybrid case exists—where an engagement ships a starter that includes a partial brand-generation engine utilizing OKLCH semantic-layer derivation scripts but is intended for deployment to one client at a time—this must be a highly conscious, documented decision rather than an unstated default. The practice's discipline requires that the team explicitly picks the shape, names it in the SOW, and strictly delivers against the constraints of that chosen shape.

## 3. The architecture decision tree — when does the engine pay back

For white-label-for-resellers engagements, the programmatic brand-generation engine is the dominant architectural cost and the defining technical capability. It represents a substantial one-time investment per portfolio that only pays off when amortized across multiple reseller tenants, similar to the programmatic derivation engines powering Adobe Spectrum 2 or the contrast-pinned generative palettes of IBM Carbon v11. The critical architectural decision for the practitioner is identifying exactly when building the engine is financially and technically warranted, and when the system is better served by manual brand configuration.

The Theme Schema serves as the binding JSON contract specifying which explicit values a brand must provide to participate in the platform. To prevent visual collapse—a failure mode where different resellers become visually indistinguishable from one another due to an under-determined brand layer—the minimum input set requires exactly five primitives. First, the primary color, which acts as the OKLCH-derived seed for the main interactive ramp, establishing the brand's primary action identity. Second, the neutral hue, serving as the gray ramp seed, which determines the overall temperature (warm vs. cool) of the surface UI. Third, a typeface stack, which is the primary conveyor of brand voice in typography. Fourth, a brand radius token, acting as the primary form-factor distinguisher (e.g., sharp corners for brutalist brands vs. heavily pill-shaped elements for approachable SaaS tools). Fifth, a brand density metric, defining the spacing rhythm that dictates the compactness or airiness of the interface.

Five primitives is the absolute floor. Adding a sixth primitive, such as a logo URI or a brand-voice tone parameter for AI-assistant copy, expands the input set and enriches the brand expression without meaningfully expanding the engineering complexity of the engine. However, going below these five inputs (e.g., asking a reseller only for a primary color and a logo) under-determines the brand layer, rendering the white-label environment visually sterile and failing to deliver on the promise of a truly brand-labeled platform.

Constructing a resilient brand-generation engine requires a meaningful initial allocation of agency resources: approximately 150–300 hours of senior design systems engineering, coupled with 80–120 hours of senior design time. This budget covers the engine logic (translating primitives into a full semantic token tree), the schema validation pipelines (ensuring inputted colors do not break WCAG 2.2 contrast minimums against generated surfaces), the onboarding interface, and the automated per-reseller QA surface.

Without an engine, the per-reseller onboarding cost is roughly equivalent to a single-brand bespoke build, as the brand layer must be hand-crafted, manually mapped to components, and manually QA'd across the entire component library. With the engine, the per-reseller onboarding cost drops precipitously to roughly 10% of the first reseller's cost, aligning with the scaling models observed in enterprise systems like IBM Carbon, where g10, g90, and g100 themes are swapped programmatically. The break-even point for the engine investment typically sits at roughly five resellers. Below five, the engine is over-engineered and the ROI is negative; at five and above, the engine pays back its initial capital and transitions into highly profitable recurring-revenue infrastructure.

To operationalize this decision-making process, the practice utilizes the following synthesis-ready artifact: an architecture decision tree that evaluates the necessity of the engine based on five constraints.

### The Architecture Decision Tree (Engine vs. No-Engine)

| Diagnostic Question | Evaluation Criteria | Architectural Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Q1**: Is the exhaustive brand list known at the time of architecture? | If all brands are known and finite, mechanical generation is unnecessary and manual curation yields higher fidelity. | Yes → No Engine (Hand-craft each brand). No → Proceed to Q2. |
| **Q2**: How many brands will launch immediately, and how many within 18 months? | Evaluates the break-even payback curve of the initial ~300-hour engineering investment. | ≤ 4 → No Engine. ≥ 5 → Engine Warranted. |
| **Q3**: Is low per-brand onboarding cost a strict commercial constraint? | Determines if the recurring revenue model relies on high-volume, low-touch tenant adoption where manual QA would destroy margins. | Yes → Engine Required. No → Engine Optional. |
| **Q4**: Does the brand layer need to vary on more than four core primitives? | If variance is strictly limited to primary color and logo, manual overrides via CSS variables may suffice. High variance demands programmatic derivation to maintain cross-token harmony. | Yes → Engine Warranted. No → Manual override works. |
| **Q5**: Will the engine be maintained by a dedicated DS engineer post-handoff, or must it be self-service? | Assesses ongoing governance. Client self-service requires heavy automated validation pipelines; dedicated agency engineering allows for lighter programmatic validation with manual oversight. | Self-Service → Engine + Heavy Validation Tooling. Dedicated → Engine + Light Validation. |

As of mid-2026, the build-vs-buy calculation for this engine heavily favors custom building. Vendors in the design systems space—such as Specify, Knapsack, Supernova, and Backlight—are increasingly shipping primitives-to-semantic-layer derivation capabilities. However, none of these platforms are fully sufficient for white-label-for-resellers at a true portfolio scale out-of-the-box. The automated contrast-matrix validation surface remains too thin, the automated per-brand QA capabilities are lacking, and the reseller onboarding pipeline almost always requires custom middleware to bridge the platform's CMS or tenant database to the token pipeline. The practice's strategic bet is that native vendor support for these N-tier white-label operations will mature in late 2026 to 2027. Until then, the brand-generation engine remains a custom engineering build, representing a meaningful, yet commercially bounded, bridge investment for the agency.

## 4. Headless-vs-opinionated for white-label — the visual-vs-behaviour layer decision

A fundamental architectural tension exists within white-label products: resellers, and bespoke clients utilizing starter foundations, demand the ability to override surface elements to express brand identity, but they must not be permitted to override core interactive behaviors. Providing full component overrides risks destroying the platform's user experience and accessibility guarantees. The architectural solution to this tension is the strict separation of the visual layer from the behavioral layer, primarily achieved through the implementation of headless-component architecture.

The architectural split must be dogmatic. The Behaviour Layer is system-owned and entirely non-negotiable. This encompasses the keyboard interaction model, focus trapping within modals, focus restoration upon dismissal, ARIA semantics, state machines for complex composite widgets (like comboboxes or date pickers), strict accessibility (a11y) contracts, RTL (right-to-left) string handling, and basic rendering logic. The platform owns this layer absolutely, and resellers cannot override it under any circumstances. The Visual Layer is reseller-owned, though strictly negotiable within the bounds of the Theme Schema. This encompasses semantic color mapping, typography, spatial density, border radius, motion duration and easing curves, iconography sets, and surface elevation. Resellers can override these values exclusively via the Theme Schema contract. The system renders what the schema dictates; the system does not explicitly design for each individual reseller.

This explicit split is what makes white-label-for-resellers commercially viable and legally defensible. If a reseller is granted the ability to override the keyboard model or focus states, they inevitably break accessibility across their instance of the platform. Resellers generally possess neither the technical expertise nor the testing infrastructure to manipulate behavioral states safely. By enforcing the split, the platform retains total liability for a11y conformance and functional robustness, while the reseller retains agency over brand expression.

In execution, the headless-component implementation pattern requires the system to ship a component library where each component is composed of a headless behavioral primitive—such as Radix UI's `<Dialog.Root>` or React Aria's `useDialog` hook—wrapped in a visual styling layer that dynamically consumes the Theme Schema. The agency builds the visual layer once against the core engine, and the engine generates the necessary CSS or tokenized output for the per-reseller visual rendering. The behavioral primitive remains universally shared across all tenants; only the visual layer is procedurally generated and swapped.

Conversely, the opinionated-component case applies specifically to white-label-as-starter engagements, which do not strictly require a headless architecture. The starter kit ships with components containing strong, opinionated visual defaults. The bespoke client engagement then manually modifies this visual layer directly during the engagement lifecycle. Opinionated components are entirely acceptable for the as-starter shape because the visual modifications occur at design time (e.g., detaching components or modifying variable collections within Figma) and during the initial application build, rather than at runtime on a per-reseller basis. Enforcing a strict headless discipline on an internal as-starter kit is often an exercise in over-engineering, adding significant architectural complexity and abstracting code without a commensurate return on investment for standard bespoke builds.

The hybrid scenario involves engagements that ship a starter utilizing headless primitives because the bespoke client engagement is explicitly expected to require cross-product brand variation or heavy architectural scaling later in its lifecycle. This hybrid is a conscious bet on future needs and must be priced accordingly, as integrating headless primitives requires a higher baseline of engineering maturity.

The tooling reality for headless architecture evolves on a quarterly basis. As of mid-2026, the core contenders dictate specific framework constraints and project trajectories:

- **Radix UI**: Remains the practice's default for React-native white-label engagements. It offers superior developer ergonomics, excellent primitive composition, and a highly granular approach to building custom design systems.
- **React Aria (Adobe)**: The premier choice for cross-framework engagements or applications where maximal accessibility depth, complex collections, and enterprise-grade internationalization (i18n) are non-negotiable product requirements.
- **Ark UI / Zag.js**: The optimal choice for engagements that require framework agnosticism, supporting Vue, Solid, and Svelte alongside React by wrapping complex behavioral logic in state machines.
- **Headless UI**: Best relegated to Tailwind-heavy projects that only require a basic, app-level shell rather than a robust, highly composable multi-tenant design system foundation.

Practitioners must verify the framework's current state and compatibility per project, as the ecosystem's momentum frequently shifts.

## 5. The commercial shape of white-label engagements

The commercial structuring of white-label engagements represents a radical departure from the practice's standard build-and-handoff design system contracts. The two white-label shapes carry distinctly different revenue mechanisms, SOW structures, and margin profiles. Articulating these differences is critical to avoiding the run-phase pricing gap identified in previous practice analyses (00d).

### Commercial Shape Comparison

| Commercial Dimension | White-Label-For-Resellers | White-Label-As-Starter (e.g., Prism2) |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary Value Proposition** | Generates recurring platform revenue through scalable, automated multi-tenant brand onboarding. | Drives internal agency velocity and margin compression on standard bespoke client builds. |
| **Architectural Baseline** | Headless primitives + Programmatic brand-generation engine + Theme Schema contract. | Opinionated or semi-opinionated components + Starter Figma kit + Baseline code foundations. |
| **Build SOW Shape** | Prices the engine (~150–300h), schema, reference library (~250–400h), onboarding pipeline (~80–150h), and first 3 resellers (~80–160h). Total hours significantly exceed standard patterns. | Prices the bespoke client build at standard hours. Compression occurs in foundation phases; handoff, audit, and dev-support phases do not compress. |
| **Run SOW (Retainer) Pattern** | SLA-backed recurring retainer covering engine uptime, brand-onboarding execution, and schema evolution coordination. | Standard bespoke maintenance (if applicable). The starter kit itself is an internal agency overhead cost. |
| **Brand-Onboarding Pricing** | Tiered per-reseller signup fees ($5k–$25k based on complexity) + Monthly engine access fee ($1k–$5k/mo). | N/A (Client pays for the standard agency engagement scope, not per-brand onboarding). |

For the white-label-for-resellers commercial shape, the Build SOW must secure the engineering proof point by pricing the foundation, the engine, the schema, and the integration of the first three reseller brands. The recurring Run SOW is the true commercial premise of the engagement. It establishes a recurring brand-onboarding retainer structured around three specific, legally binding Service Level Agreements (SLAs):

- **Brand-onboarding SLA**: A guarantee that a new reseller is fully onboarded, validated against the schema, and deployed within X days of the contract signature.
- **Engine availability SLA**: A guarantee that the derivation engine maintains 99.x% uptime and consistently generates valid, a11y-compliant output for all existing tenants.
- **Schema-evolution coordination**: Coverage for migrating existing resellers when the platform's core Theme Schema undergoes breaking changes, ensuring backward compatibility or managed upgrades.

Without these specific SLAs, a retainer is merely a vague promise of "help when needed," which fails to bind to a predictable recurring revenue number.

The load-bearing commercial decision is the brand-onboarding pricing model. The practice must tier these costs based on brand complexity, which directly maps to the input-set scope of the engine defined in Section 3:

- **Simple Brand ($5k)**: Requires only primary color, logo, and typeface. Minimal manual validation is required beyond automated checks.
- **Standard Brand ($12k)**: Adds neutral hue, brand radius, and density tokens. Requires contrast-matrix validation and layout-shift checks to ensure the spatial density does not break complex components.
- **Complex Brand ($25k+)**: Adds custom motion easing, bespoke iconography sets, and custom voice parameters. Requires heavy manual QA intervention alongside the engine to ensure visual coherence.

This recurring brand-onboarding retainer is the most concrete answer to the "run phase is unpriced" gap (00d) that the practice has surfaced. White-label-for-resellers engagements structurally force the run-phase pricing question because the programmatic engine simply cannot operate without ongoing maintenance. The practice that articulates the for-resellers retainer pattern successfully establishes a productised retainer-phase shape that can generalize to standard bespoke engagements, not just to white-label platforms.

Consequently, the for-resellers model does not fit cleanly into the practice's existing Pattern 1–5 commercial shapes. The practice must officially add a sixth pattern: **Pattern 6 — White-label / reseller platform**. The hour ranges for Pattern 6 are substantially higher than Patterns 1–5 because the engine is a heavy engineering lift, the deliverable shape relies on schema documentation rather than a single client's finished library, and the commercial structure necessitates a recurring retainer.

For the white-label-as-starter commercial shape, clients do not pay for the starter kit directly; it remains agency-internal IP. Following the current commercial position established in 00d, engagements utilizing the kit (like Prism2) are scoped at standard hours. The kit acts as a quality and consistency accelerator, not a line-item reduction for the client. The commercial benefit to the agency manifests as internal velocity—allowing the practice to execute more engagements per practitioner annually because the startup costs associated with token taxonomy setup and base component scaffolding drop dramatically.

## 6. Prism positioning closure — resolving 09 §3.2

A persistent positioning conflict has existed within the practice's corpus (identified in 09 §3.2): internal materials alternately describe Prism as a "design system" and an "internal UI Kit and set of tools," despite external decks declaring that design systems are categorically not UI kits. This positioning conflict has remained open across the full life of the knowledge base, generating friction during scoping and client presentations. This report resolves the conflict through the explicit definitions of white-label engagement shapes.

**The Resolution.** Prism2 is properly, formally, and exclusively described as a white-label-as-starter foundation and starter library. It is not a finished design system, nor is it a white-label-for-resellers platform. Prism2 lacks a programmatic brand-generation engine, lacks a formalized Theme Schema contract for validating arbitrary future brands, and lacks a per-reseller onboarding pipeline. Instead, Prism2 ships a highly structured token foundation, a comprehensive Figma component kit, and a coded counterpart in active development, explicitly designed to accelerate the early stages of a bespoke client engagement. It does not operate as a multi-tenant reseller platform.

**The Technology-Team-Adoption Constraint.** As noted in 00b, Prism2 is primarily realized as a Figma component kit, with its React component library counterpart currently lacking formal adoption by VML's broader technology teams. This technology-team-adoption gap is the load-bearing constraint that firmly locks Prism2 into the as-starter shape. Transitioning to a for-resellers model requires a brand-generation engine, strict schema validation pipelines, and an automated onboarding infrastructure—all of which are heavy, complex engineering initiatives that absolutely require deep technology-team commitment. Without engineering parity and full stack adoption, Prism2 cannot scale beyond serving as an accelerator for the design practice.

**The Prism2 to Prism3 Implication.** If the practice's strategic directive is to evolve Prism toward a true white-label-for-resellers capability—thereby unlocking recurring revenue from reseller platforms and establishing an agency-level repeatable brand-onboarding capability—Prism3 must be scoped to build the layers Prism2 currently lacks. This represents a highly meaningful investment: approximately 300–500 hours of senior engineering and 150–250 hours of senior design to build the generation engine, the schema contract, the automated validation surface, the per-reseller QA matrix, the onboarding documentation, and the runbook for the agency's own brand-onboarding team. The payback shape for this investment relies entirely on the recurring brand-onboarding revenue model detailed in Section 5.

**The Immediate Action and Client Narrative.** For the present, the practice must not attempt to force Prism2 to perform as a white-label-for-resellers platform. It must be positioned honestly and transparently as a white-label-as-starter foundation. Engagements utilizing Prism2 should be priced at standard hours (per 00d's current position), while internal metrics track the actual time savings across the first 5–10 fielded projects to build an empirical business case for Prism3.

This closure unlocks a highly specific and effective client conversation. Practitioners should replace vague positioning with exact language: *"Prism2 is our white-label-as-starter foundation. It is designed to accelerate the early stages of your bespoke build by providing mature architectural baselines, meaning we start from a higher level of fidelity than a from-scratch build. However, it is not a multi-tenant reseller platform; if your business model requires dynamic multi-brand tenancy, that engagement is an entirely different shape and requires a different architectural and commercial scope."* This clarification prevents the conflation that has caused the positioning conflict to persist.

## 7. Team, governance, failure modes, AI-readiness, and the See-also

Executing white-label engagements at an agency scale requires specific adjustments to team composition, strict governance models, and an acute awareness of platform-specific failure modes.

**Team Shape and Staffing Models.** For white-label-for-resellers engagements, the Build phase requires a specialized, senior-heavy pod. The team must include a senior DS architect (to define the derivation engine, the Theme Schema, and token composition rules), a senior DS engineer (to implement the engine and CI/CD per-platform outputs), a senior visual designer (to establish the reference brand and build the brand-validation tooling), a senior accessibility specialist (to govern the behavior layer's a11y contract and manage the cross-engine matrix per 28), and a technical project manager. The Run phase scales down in headcount but requires operational permanence: a part-time engine maintainer (typically 0.5 FTE for a portfolio of 10–30 resellers), a brand-onboarding designer (0.5 FTE for initial onboarding waves, scaling with the reseller list), and an on-call accessibility specialist for per-reseller validation.

For white-label-as-starter engagements, the team maps directly to the standard 00d composition for bespoke builds (senior visual designer, senior UX designer, design technologist, PM, and part-time accessibility specialist). However, it requires the addition of an agency-internal kit maintainer (typically 0.25 FTE aggregated across all active engagements utilizing the kit) to manage the starter kit's continuous evolution and harvest cross-engagement learnings.

**Governance Models.** White-label-as-starter governance is entirely internal to the agency. It requires strict policies on kit evolution: determining who owns updates, how per-engagement learnings are upstreamed back into the kit, and how kit version pinning is handled (per 24's brand version pinning pattern) to ensure that breaking changes to the core kit do not inadvertently destabilize active bespoke client builds.

White-label-for-resellers requires a vastly more complex dual-governance model: governance of the engine itself, and strict governance of the Theme Schema. Because the schema acts as a public API that external resellers code against, any schema modifications are, by definition, breaking changes for the resellers. Standard deprecation windows and versioning strategies (per 24 § Deprecation-as-a-window) must be strictly enforced.

**The Brand-Onboarding Runbook as Practice IP.** For for-resellers engagements, shipping a formalized brand-onboarding runbook is a critical piece of operational intellectual property. For each new tenant, the process entails:

- **Input-collection meeting (1–2 hrs)**: Capture primary color, neutral hue, typeface, radius, density, and voice tone from the reseller's brand owner.
- **Engine invocation (2–4 hrs)**: A senior DS engineer inputs the parameters into the generation engine and executes the derivation scripts to generate the semantic topology.
- **Per-reseller QA pass (4–8 hrs)**: Verify the engine's output against APCA/WCAG contrast minimums, accessibility standards, and visual harmony heuristics to ensure the brand layer has not collapsed.
- **Reseller-facing handoff (1 hr)**: Deliver the generated brand assets, the compiled platform-level documentation, and the SLA support details.

Total agency commitment ranges from 10–16 hours per reseller, allowing for significant margin capture under the tiered pricing model defined in Section 5.

**Specific Failure Modes.** White-label architectures introduce unique failure modes that do not exist in standard single-brand systems:

- **Brand-validation drift**: The engine's output passes contrast and a11y rules at the initial onboarding time. Later, the schema evolves, but the previously-validated brand drifts out of conformance. Continuous integration validation (per 32 §5 and 24 §Cross-brand drift) is load-bearing to prevent this.
- **The engine outpaces the system**: The engine introduces new features (new theming dimensions, new schema fields) faster than the reference component library is updated to consume them, resulting in resellers seeing configuration options that the components do not actually render.
- **Reseller customization outpaces the architecture**: A reseller demands an override outside the strict bounds of the schema. The agency executes a one-off custom build for that reseller that is not folded back into the main engine, permanently fracturing the architecture.
- **Brand-onboarding SLA missed**: The runbook's hour estimate fails (e.g., a complex brand consumes 40 hours instead of the estimated 10); the SLA breaks, jeopardizing the profitability of the recurring-retainer commercial shape.
- **The kit-vs-bespoke divergence (As-starter specific)**: Bespoke engagements heavily modify the kit's tokens and components, but those modifications and learnings are never upstreamed. The kit rapidly drifts from the actual fielded systems it spawned.
- **The "starter became a product" failure mode**: The internal agency kit matures to the point where an agency charges full bespoke rates for an engagement that mostly ships the kit unmodified. The commercial value-add becomes unclear. The ethical and commercial discipline requires that if a kit covers >70% of an engagement's needs, it must be scoped and honestly named as a "thin-bespoke" shape, priced lower than full bespoke.

**AI-Readiness for the Engine.** Per the AI-readiness pipeline defined in 30, the engine that generates per-reseller token sets must simultaneously generate per-reseller `.ai.json` registries. Every reseller possesses a unique AI-readable layer because their components inherit a bespoke, reseller-specific semantic token dictionary. The engine's total output set must include: token JSON, platform-specific CSS/Swift/Kotlin outputs, MDX documentation, and the `.ai.json` registry per component.

**MCP for White-Label.** Applying 34 (workstation MCPs) to the white-label space introduces a critical evolution: for-resellers engagements must eventually ship per-reseller MCP surfaces. Each reseller's MCP exposes their specific brand's token catalogue and component contract to local or platform-first AI agents. The engine generates the MCP server's configuration alongside the brand assets. As of mid-2026, no major white-label vendor natively ships per-reseller MCP endpoints out of the box, presenting a massive tactical opportunity for the practice to build this integration as proprietary middleware prior to native vendor adoption expected in 2027.
