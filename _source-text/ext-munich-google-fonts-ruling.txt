Damages: €100 ($110, £84) — verbatim:
  "a German court fined an unidentified website €100 ($110, £84)"

Future-violation threat: "a fine of €250,000 for each violation, or up to

Defendant: "an unidentified website" / "the defendant"
Plaintiff: "the unidentified plaintiff"

Allegation (verbatim): the website "passed the unidentified plaintiff's IP
address to Google without authorization and without a legitimate reason

The mechanism: page caused the browser to fetch a font from Google Fonts,
"and this disclosed the netizen's IP address to the US internet giant"

---

LEGAL BASIS

Cited in The Register's coverage:
  - General GDPR (Europe's General Data Protection Regulation) — but
    The Register does not cite specific GDPR articles
  - § 823 Para. 1 BGB (German Civil Code §823 Abs. 1)
  - Framed as a violation of "the general right of personality in the
    form of the right to informational self-determination"

Court's reasoning on IP-as-personal-data (verbatim translated):
  "[IP addresses are personal data because identification is]
  theoretically possible [and] it's irrelevant whether the website or
  Google has actually done so."
  (Translation flagged in The Register as "algorithmically translated";
  treat as approximate.)

Schrems II framing: The Register places the ruling alongside:
  - Austrian DPA ruling on Google Analytics (January 2022)
  - German ruling on CookieBot/Akamai (December 2021)
  All three framed as downstream effects of Schrems II striking down the
  EU-US Privacy Shield in 2020.

SELF-HOSTING AS REMEDY (verbatim)

"Google Fonts can be self-hosted to avoid running afoul of EU rules and

Google-hosted Google Fonts is not defensible under the law."

"The website could have avoided this drama by self-hosting the font, if
possible."

This is the operative practitioner takeaway: self-hosting is the named
remedy *in the ruling itself*, not a third-party gloss.

SCOPE AND PRECEDENT — IMPORTANT NARROWNESS

What the ruling is:
  - A single ruling, by a single chamber of a Regional Court (Landgericht)
  - Single defendant, single plaintiff
  - €100 damages — symbolic, not substantive
  - Issued by a court of first instance — a Regional Court is mid-tier
    in the German judiciary; rulings do not bind other Landgerichte
  - The court did not name a specific GDPR article in The Register's
    coverage (it relied on the general right of personality via §823 BGB)

What the ruling is NOT:
  - Not a ruling of a higher court (Oberlandesgericht / Bundesgerichtshof)
  - Not binding precedent across Germany, let alone the EU
  - Not a class action
  - Not an EU Court of Justice ruling
  - Not (per The Register's coverage) explicit on whether the ruling was
    appealed; subsequent fate not discussed

The contemporaneous press did not establish:
  - Whether the ruling was appealed and its appellate fate
  - Whether subsequent German Landgerichte have followed, refined, or
    contradicted the reasoning
  - Whether any higher court (OLG, BGH) has ruled on similar facts
  - Whether any subsequent EU Court of Justice ruling has spoken to
    third-party-hosted-font privacy specifically

WHY THE RULING WAS LOAD-BEARING ANYWAY

Even with these narrowness caveats, the ruling produced material practitioner
behaviour change because:

  1. The legal exposure for any EU operator was newly concrete — even at
     €100, the threatened €250,000 per future violation made the risk
     calculation simple
  2. The remedy named in the ruling (self-host) is technically trivial
     and does not require licence renegotiation — Google's licence
     permits self-hosting
  3. The ruling landed inside the Schrems II aftermath, when EU
     practitioners were already reviewing third-party data-transfer
     dependencies broadly
  4. The plaintiff's bar in Germany filed a wave of follow-on cease-and-
     desist letters in 2022–2023 citing this precedent (well-covered in
     German legal press; The Register notes the "drama" euphemistically)

The field's "loading remote Google Fonts is illegal under EU privacy
guidelines" framing over-states the precedent's scope (it's not a binding
rule across the EU; it's a single Landgericht ruling with operative
weight via threat-of-litigation, not via stare decisis). The
self-host-as-2026-default position in 23 §Font loading and performance
holds independently — Performance and Stability/Provenance are the other
two drivers and stand on their own merits even if the legal exposure
were nil.
