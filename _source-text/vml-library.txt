   The best design systems are simply a force multiplier for what’s already happening inside an
organization. Those systems refactor the already established practices and patterns of the org so
they can be used everywhere. If your organization has healthy practices and patterns, your design
system can help scale those across teams/brands/channels/etc. If your organization has unhealthy
     practices, expecting a design system alone to fix those practices is misplaced optimism.

  Design systems distill and magnify healthy practices and patterns
    while exposing and challenging unhealthy ones. The amount of
resistance a healthy design system team experiences is directly related
to the unhealthiness of the organization’s established ways of working.

Balancing
Centralization and
Brand Independence
One of the most significant challenges in scaling multi-brand
design systems is finding the right balance between
centralized efficiency and brand independence. Organizations
typically adopt one of three approaches:


1. Fully centralized: A single core system with theming
   capabilities for brand-specific visual styles
2. Federated: A central system with defined extension
   points where brands can customize components
3. Distributed with governance: Brand-specific
   systems that adhere to shared principles and
   standards
Governance Best
Practices
Define Clear Roles
Explicitly stating who owns, decides, and contributes helps everyone understand their role
within the system.


Establish Intake & Contribution Processes
Creating straightforward workflows for contributing or proposing changes is essential.


Implement Version Control
Proper version control through semantic versioning helps track changes systematically.
Maintaining a changelog and developing a strategy for phasing out deprecated
components keeps the system current while supporting legacy implementations.


Regular Reviews and Communications
Scheduled meetings with design, engineering, and product teams enable collaborative
decision-making. Monthly or quarterly organization-wide updates maintain awareness and
buy-in across the company.

Component Testing
Process
• Content – can this component or variation handle a variety of content situations
  (such as lengthy or internationalized text)?
• Accessibility – does this component meet or exceed accessibility requirements and follow accessibility
  guidelines?
• Cross-browser/device – test across supported browsers and devices
• Responsive – test across the entire resolution spectrum to ensure proper display on any screen size
• Functionality – unit testing for functionality
• Create stress test examples in the workshop to capture common, edge, and stress scenarios
• Internal code review – ensure component code meets design system coding standards
• Internal design review – ensure work adheres to the design language and meets all design
  requirements
• Trial in applications – It may be helpful to collaborate with user devs to test a pre-release of the
  component in applications to ensure things work properly, API endpoints are intuitive, etc.
• Any other internal QA and testing

OPPORTUNITY
Inconsistent and inaccessible digital experiences were leading to
frustration for Wendy’s users. These issues stemmed from a lack of
standardization across it’s products. In addition to a poor experience, the
fragmentation meant updates and expansions were unnecessarily time-
consuming and resource-intensive, translating to a slow time-to-market
and increased costs.

SOLUTION
Wendy’s Fresh Design System was created to provide an accessible, reusable
standard to support a rebuild of the existing mobile iOS & Android apps into a
single code-base in Flutter. In doing so it has allowed a reduction of 4000 lines
of code in the app, and helped increase time-to-market speeds by 600%. It has
since expanded to support Wendy’s digital ordering website as well as the CRM
platform. It also provides usage guidelines as a reference for best practices.

Balancing
Centralization and
Brand Independence
One of the most significant challenges in scaling multi-brand
design systems is finding the right balance between
centralized efficiency and brand independence. Organizations
typically adopt one of three approaches:


1. Fully centralized: A single core system with theming
   capabilities for brand-specific visual styles
2. Federated: A central system with defined extension
   points where brands can customize components
3. Distributed with governance: Brand-specific
   systems that adhere to shared principles and
   standards


Recommendation: Centralized Model
A centralized model features a dedicated team responsible for the design
system's development and maintenance. This approach provides clear ownership
and accountability but may sometimes disconnect from day-to-day product
needs if not carefully managed.

Governance Best
Practices
Define Clear Roles
Explicitly stating who owns, decides, and contributes helps everyone understand their role
within the system.


Establish Intake & Contribution Processes
Creating straightforward workflows for contributing or proposing changes is essential.


Implement Version Control
Proper version control through semantic versioning helps track changes systematically.
Maintaining a changelog and developing a strategy for phasing out deprecated
components keeps the system current while supporting legacy implementations.


Regular Reviews and Communications
Scheduled meetings with design, engineering, and product teams enable collaborative
decision-making. Monthly or quarterly organization-wide updates maintain awareness and
buy-in across the company.

Component
Qualification
Establishing clear criteria for what qualifies as a system
component prevents bloat and maintains quality. This
process should define:



• The problem the component solves
• How it differs from existing components
• Its reusability potential across brands
• Technical feasibility and accessibility compliance
Component Request
and Intake Process
A clear intake process ensures product teams know how
to request new components, while maintaining design
system quality and minimizing unnecessary one-offs.
Component Testing
Process
• Content – can this component or variation handle a variety of content situations
  (such as lengthy or internationalized text)?
• Accessibility – does this component meet or exceed accessibility requirements and follow accessibility
  guidelines?
• Cross-browser/device – test across supported browsers and devices
• Responsive – test across the entire resolution spectrum to ensure proper display on any screen size
• Functionality – unit testing for functionality
• Create stress test examples in the workshop to capture common, edge, and stress scenarios
• Internal code review – ensure component code meets design system coding standards
• Internal design review – ensure work adheres to the design language and meets all design
  requirements
• Trial in applications – It may be helpful to collaborate with user devs to test a pre-release of the
  component in applications to ensure things work properly, API endpoints are intuitive, etc.
• Any other internal QA and testing

Workflow
The component contribution process should follow a
structured workflow guiding how team members propose,

typically involves:

• Proposal submission with use case justification
• Design and development specifications
• Peer review for quality assurance
• Testing across brands for compatibility
• Documentation creation
• Final approval and implementation
• Distribution and announcement to system users
