Hot Potato Process — verbatim definition from Mall's post:
> "The big misconception I've seen designers and developers often fall victim to is believing that handoff goes one way. Designers hand off comps to developers and think their work is done. That puts a lot of pressure on the designer to get everything perfect in one pass.
>
> Instead, great collaboration follows what Brad Frost and I call 'The Hot Potato Process,' where ideas are passed quickly back and forth from designer to developer and back to designer then back to developer for the entirety of a product creation cycle."

Operationalisation guidance (verbatim section headings):
- "Sit together" — the simplest entry point; "many a designer + developer pair who have worked together for years become enlightened as to how the other works within the first few minutes of sitting together."
- "Distributed teams" — use video chat as a co-located proxy; "leave a Zoom chat open for a few hours as a proxy for being in the same office together, even if we're not talking to each other the whole time." Cites Mandy Brown's "Making remote teams work" essay.
- "Use recordings to bridge time zone gaps" — when synchronous isn't possible, record yourself working and pass it to the collaborator who continues the work and records back. Tools named: Voxer, Marco Polo. The walkie-talkie/intercom shape lets asynchronous collaboration approximate synchronous.
- "Working together means working together" — closing rule: if you can't sit together, trade recordings, or approximate any of these, "you might have to come to terms with the fact that you're not truly working together."
