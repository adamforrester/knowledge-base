        Designers will train systems, so menial reviews are no longer required



For years, brand guidelines have been contained in documents that teams couldnˇt find or didnˇt have time to reference when they needed to move fast. The result was that designers, who had to manually review everything, make corrections, and approve each asset one by one, would face an ever-growing backlog of requests.€
Designers are now able to encode brand rules such as logo clearspace requirements, approved color pairings, typography hierarchy, and tone of voice into AI design systems. The system can enforce the embedded logic automatically. Whenever a marketer or salesperson has a design request, instead of asking a designer or waiting for approval, they can simply prompt their systemˇs design copilot to generate on-brand assets. Designers wonˇt need to make the assets themselves.

                        Designers will set the rules in the tools by defining what ˝on-brand˛ means, in systematic terms that AI can understand. Designers will need to teach the system to enforce rules such as acceptable color combinations, tone of voice, and visual styles for product photography. They will also train the system with examples, by providing reference images, as well as approving layouts and art direction, so the system learns what good looks like for their specific brand. As a brand evolves, the system will also need maintenance and ongoing training to be effective.

         Designers will spend more time on strategic activities



Generative AI tools can serve as a copilot and proactively generate assets. However, with such an abundance of options, thereˇs a greater need for clear judgment and good taste. If thereˇs a cultural moment that comes up, which creates an opportunity for commentary, whatˇs the best thing for the brand to say? If thereˇs an important sales meeting, which specific asset works best?
This ambiguity is where brand designers remain indispensable. Storytelling is nuanced. Things need to feel right; rules constantly need updating in a dynamic world. What may have felt right six months 
                                                                      ago might feel tone-deaf in the present moment. As culture shifts, and brand needs evolve, the designer needs to continue the story and update the system accordingly. They need to make sure that outputs still feel authentic to the brand.
At heart, designers have always been storytellers first. Graphics and images are tools to tell a story about what a product does or how it makes someone feel.

                                       The shift from generation to systems represents a maturation of how organizations think about AIˇs role in creative work. Teams are moving past AIˇs generative capabilities, to the far more consequential issues of how AI fits into the teamˇs workflows. For brand teams, this means:

Evaluating tools based on governance and control, not just output quality
Building systems that enable the organization while maintaining brand integrity
Evolving the designerˇs role away from execution, towards strategic judgment and system architecture

The teams successfully deploying AI in production arenˇt the ones with the most sophisticated prompts or the largest budgets. Theyˇre the ones who have figured out how to encode their brand rules into systems, establish clear governance frameworks, and position designers to do what they do best: exercise taste, tell stories, and make judgment calls that keep the brand authentic.
