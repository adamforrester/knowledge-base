Governance Strategies for Multi-Brand
Design Systems: Building Consistency
Across          Boundaries
A multi-brand design system is a centralized set of guidelines, assets, and rules that
enables consistent user interfaces across multiple brands or sub-brands within a larger
organization.
Unlike standard design systems, multi-brand systems are optimized for flexibility, allowing
product teams to leverage an existing component library while adapting it to different
brand identities
The Governance Challenge
As design systems gain traction, multiple activities occur simultaneously-some
contributors may add elements without checking in, others voice opinions on components,
and teams request formal contribution processes. Without proper governance, this
enthusiasm can quickly erode confidence in the system, making decisions seem arbitrary
rather than methodical.
Recommended Governance Model for Multi-Brand
Design Systems
Centralized Governance Model
A centralized model features a dedicated team responsible for the design system's
development and maintenance. This approach provides clear ownership and accountability
but may sometimes disconnect from day-to-day product needs if not carefully managed.
Alternative: Hybrid Governance Model
Many organizations ultimately adopt a hybrid approach, combining centralized oversight
with distributed contribution. This balances consistency with practical implementation
concerns and offers flexibility as the organization grows.
Establishing Governance Best Practices
Defining Clear Roles and Responsibilities
Explicitly stating who owns, decides, and contributes helps everyone understand their role
within the system. Clear role definition prevents confusion and establishes accountability
across the organization.
Establishing Contribution Processes
Creating straightforward workflows for proposing changes is essential. Tools like GitHub or
Figma can facilitate this process, while setting clear criteria for new additions keeps the
system organized. A structured contribution model should outline:
 • Who can contribute
 • What qualifies for a contribution
 • How to progress from proposal to implementation
This standardization ensures the design system evolves consistently and controllably.
Implementing Version Control
Proper version control through semantic versioning helps track changes systematically.
Maintaining a changelog and developing a strategy for phasing out deprecated
components keeps the system current while supporting legacy implementations.
Regular Reviews and Communications
Scheduled meetings with design, engineering, and product teams enable collaborative
decision-making. Monthly or quarterly organization-wide updates maintain awareness and
buy-in across the company.

Component               Governance               Strategies
A structured intake and evaluation process ensures that new component requests are
aligned with system goals, maintain quality standards, and support scalable multi-brand
growth.
Component Qualification Process
Establishing clear criteria for what qualifies as a system component prevents bloat and
maintains quality. This process should define:
 1. The problem the component solves
 2. How it differs from existing components
 3. Its reusability potential across brands
 4. Technical feasibility and accessibility compliance
Component Request and Intake Process
A clear intake process ensures product teams know how to request new components, while
maintaining design system quality and minimizing unnecessary one-offs.
Component Request and Intake Steps:
 1. Initial Inquiry: Teams first review the design system catalog to confirm no existing
     component meets their needs.
 2. Communication Channel: If no solution exists, teams submit a request through a
     designated intake channel (e.g., Slack, Jira, GitHub).
 3. Assessment and Guidance: The design system team collaborates with the requestor
     to assess needs and recommend adaptations or new work.
 4. Qualification Review: Requests are evaluated based on cross-brand reusability,
     system alignment, technical feasibility, and future scalability.
 5. Prototyping and Initial Concept: A lightweight prototype or mockup is created to
     visualize the problem and proposed solution.
 6. Review and Approval: The design system and product teams jointly review and
     approve solutions based on quality and system fit.
 7. Prioritization and Backlog Entry: Approved component work is added to the design
     system backlog and prioritized based on impact and urgency.
 8. Formal Development and Testing: The component proceeds through formal design,
     engineering, and system-level testing.
 9. Documentation and Release: The finalized component is documented, integrated
     into the system, and included in the next version release.
Component Testing Process
 1. Content – can this component or variation handle a variety of content situations
     (such as lengthy or internationalized text)?
 2. Accessibility – does this component meet or exceed accessibility requirements and
    follow accessibility guidelines?
 3. Cross-browser/device – test across supported browsers and devices
 4. Responsive – test across the entire resolution spectrum to ensure proper display on
    any screen size
 5. Functionality – unit testing for functionality
 6. Create stress test examples in the workshop to capture common, edge, and stress
    scenarios
 7. Internal code review – ensure component code meets design system
    coding standards
 8. Internal design review – ensure work adheres to the design language and meets all
    design requirements
 9. Trial in applications – It may be helpful to collaborate with user devs to test a pre-
    release of the component in applications to ensure things work properly, API
    endpoints are intuitive, etc.
10. Any other internal QA and testing

Contribution Workflow
The component contribution process should follow a structured workflow guiding how
team members propose, develop, review, and integrate new components. This typically
involves:
  1. Proposal submission with use case justification
  2. Design and development specifications
  3. Peer review for quality assurance
  4. Testing across brands for compatibility
  5. Documentation creation
  6. Final approval and implementation
  7. Distribution and announcement to system users
Documentation Governance
Design system documentation is where a product team records the rules of its visual and
interactive language, serving as the reference point for how things should look, behave,
and be built.
Documentation Structure
Effective documentation typically includes specifications for typography, color, spacing,
icons, components, and interface patterns3. As systems mature, documentation often
expands to include code snippets, usage examples, and contribution guidelines3.
Documentation Standards
Establishing clear standards for documentation ensures consistency and usability:
 1. Writing guidelines for tone and style
 2. Required information for each component
 3. Example formats and implementation details
 4. Accessibility requirements documentation
 5. Brand-specific variations and guidelines
Maintenance Responsibility
Documentation responsibilities should be clearly assigned, whether to a centralized team,
component owners, or through a federated model where representatives from each brand
maintain their specific sections.
Accessibility Governance
Accessibility is a foundational element of any design system, requiring dedicated
governance attention to ensure compliance across brands.
Setting Accessibility Standards
While WCAG 2.0 Level AA compliance is the baseline for legal requirements, organizations
should strive for WCAG 2.1 AA or even the newer WCAG 2.2 standards for comprehensive
accessibility.
Testing and Verification
Centralized accessibility testing provides consistency, but individual brand
implementations must also conduct their own testing. Governance structures should
define:
 1. Required accessibility testing procedures
 2. Documentation of accessibility features
 3. Process for addressing accessibility issues
 4. Regular accessibility audits across brands
Conclusion
Governing a multi-brand design system requires balancing consistency with flexibility,
maintaining quality while enabling efficient contributions, and ensuring accessibility while
supporting brand individuality. The governance approach should evolve alongside
organizational growth and system maturity.
The most effective governance strategies combine clear processes, defined roles, regular
communication, and measurable outcomes. By implementing structured governance
across components, documentation, and accessibility concerns, Hub can realize the full
benefits of multi-brand design systems: reduced costs, increased development efficiency,
faster time to market, and consistent user experiences that still maintain brand identity.
Governance is a critical factor determining whether they truly deliver on their promise of
unified, consistent experiences across multiple brands while still allowing each brand's
unique personality to shine.
